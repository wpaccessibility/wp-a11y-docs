# Accessibility legislation

Accessibility legislation is a complicated topic. We will try to cover the most important subjects, but if you have any questions about legislation for your situation, please find legal help with an expert firm. On this page we will address the Americans with Disabilities Act, Section 508, the European Accessibility Act, the Web Accessibility Directive, and EN 301 549.

The Web Accessibility Initiative maintains a [list of laws and policies by country](https://www.w3.org/WAI/policies/).

In most countries or unions there are laws that state you must build accessible digital products and services. Which laws apply to you depends on whom you're selling the product or service to. That's why in most legislation, the same basis for adhering to it is used: the WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.

For example, if you sell to customers in the U.S., you need to look at the Americans with Disabilities Act. For the European Union, the European Accessibility Act applies. Both mention the WCAG as the technical standard for accessibility. Other than meeting those standards, you may have other obligations as well.

If you are building a WordPress plugin or theme, your software may be used in any jurisdiction. Customers may need your application to meet their local standards to legally use it.

## The Americans with Disabilities Act

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) governs accessibility requirements in the United States of America. The law, established in 1991, doesn't specifically address the internet or websites, but does address many key concepts surrounding operating a business or service with accessibility requirements.

The general interpretation of the law is that businesses are required to provide accessible services, but the specific details are largely dictated by the courts. Government, health care and many publicly-funded organizations (such as schools) have specific guidance, but businesses do not.

The lack of specific guidance should not be interpreted as a lack of a requirement for accessibility. There are plenty of cases already published where either consumers or others made a successful complaint in court, even to companies outside the U.S.

Learn more [about the American with Disabilities Act](https://www.ada.gov/)

## Section 508

Another law in the U.S. is Section 508, a federal law and an amendment to the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. This mandates federal agencies and their contractors to ensure their ICT is accessible to people with disabilities. Section 508 still uses WCAG 2.0 as a basis for their technical standard. If you are a contractor for a federal agency, this is what you need to look at. However, since WCAG has been updated with newer guidelines, it doesn't hurt to use WCAG 2.1 or 2.2 as a standard for your projects.

Learn more [about Section 508](https://www.section508.gov/)

## The European Accessibility Act

The European Accessibility Act (EAA) is a Directive to member states, who then implement it locally. The EAA describes accessibility requirements for products and services sold to consumers within the European Union. This includes websites and apps, but also other types of software and digital displays. This includes websites and apps, but also other types of software and digital displays, as well as PDFs like user manuals.

EU member states agreed to add these requirements in their legislation.

The EAA is not itself legislation. Instead, it imposes a requirement on the European member states that they must establish accessibility laws. These laws must cover certain specific areas, but the individual laws are variable. It's a common misconception that if you're not based in the EU you don't have to adhere. If you are based outside the EU, but you sell products or services to Europe, you're still required to make those products and services accessible.

Note that laws of many EU nations require more than just accessibility of the site or app, but also things like accessibility statements with specific content (including known remaining barriers in some cases), and self-reporting of barriers to oversight agencies.

Learn more [about the European Accessibility Act](https://equalizedigital.com/european-accessibility-act/).

## Web Accessibility Directive

Section 508 in the U.S. is comparable to the Web Accessibility Directive (WAD) in Europe. As with the European Accessibility Act, this is not a law but also a guideline to put accessibility into legislation. In most European countries the WAD has turned into legislation since 2018. It's focused on government and semi-governmental organizations, as well as their contractors.

You need to have an accessibility statement on your website, which is updated yearly with the progress of the work you did to become fully accessible. Other requirements could differ per country.

Learn more [about Web Accessibility Directive](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/web-accessibility-directive-standards-and-harmonisation).

## EN 301 549

In Europe, the WAD as well as the EAA point to accessibility requirements for ICT products and services, as published in a document called EN 301 549. The EN stands for European Norm. It draws heavily from the WCAG 2.1, adapted for specific needs per country. In this document you will find accessibility guidelines for everything from websites to closed software. The [Eurepean Commmission has mentioned that EU member states are free to use WCAG 2.2](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/web-accessibility-directive-standards-and-harmonisation) if they wish.

{> .callout .warning}
Fair warning: meeting all the success criteria for WCAG 2.1 or 2.2 will not ensure presumption of conformity with WAD or EAA, since EN 301 549 has extra requirements. So it's an important document to keep an eye on.

Find the most [recent version of EN 301 549 (pdf)](https://www.etsi.org/deliver/etsi_en/301500_301599/301549/03.02.01_60/en_301549v030201p.pdf)

## Global Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

In the absence of specific legal guidelines, it is most commonly recommended that you follow the [latest version Web Content Accessibility Guidelines](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/). WCAG is intended as a multipurpose document that can apply to any website or app.
